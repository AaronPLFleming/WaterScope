Testing of Solution (Monica)

Tests on both pressure and turbidity were performed on the final design in order to obtain some quantitative results on its performance.

It is important to note that the following results are meant to give a rough idea of a relationship between the prototype performance, vacuum pressure and water turbidity; it is not meant to be conclusive as not all variables could not be controlled to laboratory standard. For example, the composition of tap water can vary depending on the temperature of the water or nature of the pipes. However, tap water was the most convenient source to use and was sufficiently different to Cam water to make some meaningful conclusions.

Pressure

To test how pressure affects the time it takes to pull water through the filter, the prototype was tested with 100 mL of tap water at an 8, 10 and 12 psi negative pressure difference from atmospheric pressure, which is about 14.7 psi.

The graphs and table below show the results of these tests. The curves on the left seem to follow a linear relationship; however, it can be seen that over time, the flow rate through the filter decreases, which is likely due to impurities in the tap water clogging up the filter.
The graph on the right suggests that there is approximately a 1 minute increase in time per unit psi decrease in pressure difference. However, this is a very loose assertion as there are only three data points, but the aim here is just to give some rough numbers on the relationship between pressure and time.

Pressure difference (psi) Time to filter 100 mL of tap water
12 9 min 33 s
10 11 min 28 s
8 13 min 0 s
Syringe Method 8 min 45 s

As expected, the 12 psi pressure difference resulted in the quickest time to filter 100 mL of tap water. Although WaterScope's current method using the syringe took less time to filter the water, our current prototype has many advantages over WaterScope's current design, as explained in the previous section and in the End User Perspective section.
