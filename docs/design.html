Design

Aims and Requirements (Katie)

Our aim was to create a user-friendly, modular water filtering system which could directly replace the current system used by WaterScope. Our system should be quicker, cheaper and easier to use.

Four key requirements of the system were identified. The system should:

  1. Be compatible with the current WaterScope filter and testing process, to make adoption of the new system as easy as possible.
  2. Cost less than £0.50 per use, in order for the system to be sustainable if a large number of tests are required.
  3. Filter 100ml of water in less than 15 minutes, to be able to deliver speedy results on the water.
  4. Require minimal physical effort, so that access to the system is not limited by the strength of the user.

Additionally, it would be an advantage if the system was:

  • Easy to assemble
  • Constructed of basic parts, available in country
  • Robust and durable
  • Intuitive and simple to use
  • Modular, with highly standardised interfaces
  • Able to filter multiple water sources at once
  • Able to work autonomously after initial set-up

In addition to designing the system based on the requirements and wishes described above, the system was developed using a very user-centred design process, based around the Unicef ‘Principles for Design’. More details of these can be found here.

Initial Ideas (Sajan)

After formulating the problem and design criteria, we initially considered two broad approaches to solve the problem:

  • A positive pressure device - similar to the syringe being used by WaterScope, but one optimised to apply a greater force and thus increase the flow rate.
  • A vacuum filtering system - involving a low cost sterile disposable container above the filter to hold the sample water, a vacuum pump, and a collection vessel downstream of the filter.

It was envisaged that the latter approach would likely have more potential, as the only disposable element of the design would be the low cost container. Furthermore, the collection vessel and vacuum pump could be adapted from existing materials in country, and system operation would require minimal physical effort.
In contrast, using a positive pressure method would still require physical effort, and the cost of the disposable syringe would likely increase if it were optimised such that a greater force could be applied.

After this decision, we began by considering the minimisaton of the cost of the disposable container, by thinking of the simplest container we could, a plastic bag.
Two different approaches beckoned:

  • Using a very thin plastic tube supported by a funnel which interfaced with the filter.
  • Using a more rigid but costly container to hold the sample water, without the need for a supporting funnel.

3D sketches of these approaches were created using CAD software and are displayed below.

Three principal advantages of the latter design made us choose this approach as the basis for our prototypes.

  • The container interface with the filter could be made robust, thus ensuring a tight seal necessary for the vacuum filtration.
  • In the long term the container could be adapted to both collect and filter the sample, thus minimising the number of sterile disposable containers needed.
  • It is difficult to guarantee that the funnel remains sterile after a sample is run through the system, and any contamination would require sterilisation or disposal of this high cost part.
